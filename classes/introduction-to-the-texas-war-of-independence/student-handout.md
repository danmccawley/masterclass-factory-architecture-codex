## Learning Objectives
- TLO: Describe the historical context and key events of the Texas War of Independence, including its causes, major battles, and outcomes.
- ELO: Explain the geopolitical situation in Texas prior to the war, including settlement patterns, Mexican laws, and tensions over slavery.
- ELO: Identify and describe major political and military figures involved in the conflict, such as Sam Houston and Antonio López de Santa Anna.
- ELO: Discuss the roles and perspectives of settlers, indigenous peoples, and other groups during the Texas War of Independence.
- ELO: Analyze the significance of the Texas Declaration of Independence and the formation of the Republic of Texas.
- ELO: Evaluate contested issues such as the role of slavery, racial discrimination, and differing narratives from Texian, Mexican, and indigenous viewpoints.

## Key Terms
- texas war of independence: A conflict from October 1835 to April 1836 in which Texian settlers and allies fought to gain independence from Mexico, culminating in the creation of the Republic of Texas. Why it matters: Understanding this war explains how Texas separated from Mexico and the origins of its independent government.
- texian: A term used to describe Anglo-American settlers in Mexican Texas who participated in the independence movement. Why it matters: Knowing who the Texians were helps clarify the cultural and political forces driving the conflict.
- sam houston: Commander of the Texian army who led forces to victory at the Battle of San Jacinto and later became a key political leader in Texas. Why it matters: Recognizing Houston’s role is essential to understanding Texian military success and leadership.
- antonio lópez de santa anna: Mexican general and president who led Mexican forces against Texian rebels during the war. Why it matters: Santa Anna’s leadership shaped the Mexican response and the war’s military campaigns.
- battle of gonzales: The first military engagement of the Texas War of Independence in October 1835, marked by Texian resistance to Mexican attempts to reclaim a cannon. Why it matters: This battle symbolizes the start of armed conflict and Texian defiance.
- battle of san jacinto: The decisive battle in April 1836 where Texian forces defeated the Mexican army, securing Texas independence. Why it matters: This victory ended the war and established Texian control.
- texas declaration of independence: A document adopted in March 1836 declaring Texas independent from Mexico and establishing a new government. Why it matters: It formalized the Texian cause and laid the foundation for the Republic of Texas.
- republic of texas: The independent nation established after the Texas War of Independence, existing from 1836 to 1845. Why it matters: Understanding the Republic explains Texas’s political status before joining the United States.
- empresario: A person granted the right by the Mexican government to settle families in Texas, such as Stephen F. Austin. Why it matters: Empresarios facilitated Anglo-American settlement, which influenced tensions leading to war.
- slavery in texas: The practice of enslaving people, which was brought by many southern U.S. settlers into Texas despite Mexican laws against it. Why it matters: Slavery was a major source of conflict and shaped social and political divisions in the war.
- tejanos: Mexican residents of Texas who had lived there before Anglo settlement and whose loyalties were divided during the war. Why it matters: Tejanos’ experiences highlight the complex social dynamics and contested identities in the conflict.
- indigenous peoples: Native groups living in Texas during the period, whose perspectives and roles in the war are less documented. Why it matters: Including indigenous viewpoints is important for a fuller understanding of the region’s history.

- source verification: An independent check that claims and citations are supported by the approved corpus. Why it matters: It keeps fabricated or unsupported material out of the class.
