## Learning Objectives
- TLO: Explain the significance of the Texas War of Independence, including its causes, key events, major figures, and outcomes within the broader Mexican civil war context.
- ELO: Identify the geographical and political context of Texas before the war, including Mexican colonization policies and cultural tensions.
- ELO: Describe the key players involved in the early stages of the conflict, including Anglo-American settlers, Tejanos, and Mexican government leaders.
- ELO: Analyze the major battles and political actions during the Texas Revolution, such as the Battle of Gonzales, the Siege of the Alamo, and the adoption of the Texas Declaration of Independence.
- ELO: Evaluate the diverse perspectives and contested issues surrounding the war, including the roles of slavery, citizenship rights, and internal Texan divisions.
- ELO: Interpret primary sources related to the Texas Revolution, including the Texas Declaration of Independence and the Republic of Texas Constitution.

## Key Terms
- texas war of independence: A conflict from 1835 to 1836 where Anglo-American settlers and Tejanos in Mexican Texas fought against the Mexican centralist government led by Santa Anna, resulting in Texian victory and independence. Why it matters: Understanding this war explains how Texas became an independent republic and highlights the tensions between settlers and Mexican authorities.
- tejanos: Mexican residents of Texas who lived in the region before and during the Texas Revolution, some of whom supported independence while others remained loyal to Mexico. Why it matters: Recognizing Tejanos shows the diverse local perspectives and internal divisions within Texas during the conflict.
- santa anna: Antonio López de Santa Anna was the Mexican president and general who led the centralist government forces against the Texian rebels during the Texas Revolution. Why it matters: Santa Anna’s leadership and policies were central causes of the conflict and his capture marked the war’s end.
- battle of gonzales: The first military engagement of the Texas Revolution in 1835, where Texian settlers resisted Mexican troops attempting to retrieve a cannon. Why it matters: This battle ignited the armed conflict and symbolized Texian resistance to Mexican control.
- siege of the alamo: A 13-day siege in 1836 where Mexican forces attacked Texian defenders at the Alamo mission, resulting in the death of all Texian fighters. Why it matters: The Alamo became a powerful symbol of Texian bravery and sacrifice during the revolution.
- texas declaration of independence: A document adopted on March 2, 1836, declaring Texas independent from Mexico and establishing a provisional government and constitution. Why it matters: It formally announced Texas’s break from Mexico and set the legal foundation for the new republic.
- republic of texas: The independent nation established after the Texas Revolution, existing from 1836 until Texas joined the United States in 1845. Why it matters: Knowing about the Republic explains Texas’s unique status and governance after the war.
- mexican centralist government: The political regime under Santa Anna that centralized power in Mexico City, reducing states’ autonomy and provoking unrest in regions like Texas. Why it matters: This government’s policies triggered resistance and rebellion in Texas.
- anglo-american settlers: Immigrants from the United States who settled in Mexican Texas, often ignoring Mexican laws on language, religion, and slavery. Why it matters: Their presence and cultural differences contributed to tensions leading to the revolution.
- slavery in texas: The practice of enslaving African Americans, which Anglo settlers brought to Texas despite Mexican laws restricting slavery. Why it matters: Slavery was a major social and legal conflict that influenced the war and the new Texas government.
- battle of san jacinto: The decisive 1836 battle where Texian forces led by Sam Houston defeated Santa Anna’s army and captured him, effectively ending the war. Why it matters: This victory secured Texian independence and shaped Texas’s future.
- sam houston: Commander of the Texian army who led forces to victory at San Jacinto and later became a key political leader in Texas. Why it matters: Houston’s leadership was crucial to the success of the revolution and Texas’s early governance.
- tejas (texas) in coahuila y tejas: The Mexican state combining Texas and Coahuila before the revolution, governed under Mexican federalism. Why it matters: Understanding this political unit clarifies Texas’s legal status before independence.
- federalists vs. centralists: The Mexican political conflict between those favoring state autonomy (federalists) and those supporting centralized national control (centralists). Why it matters: This broader Mexican civil war context influenced the Texas Revolution’s causes and alliances.
- treaty of velasco: Agreements signed in 1836 after Santa Anna’s capture, intended to end hostilities and recognize Texas independence (though Mexico did not fully accept them). Why it matters: The treaty marks the formal end of the war and the start of Texas as a separate entity.
