# Student Handout: Introduction to the Texas War of Independence

Class tier: Standard class
Bernard support: ask Bernard for clarification, examples, translation, quiz help, or a deeper explanation. Bernard must stay inside the approved source boundary.

## Learning Objectives
- TLO: Explain the significance of the Texas War of Independence, including its causes, key events, major figures, and outcomes.
- ELO: Identify the geographical and political context of Texas before the war, including its status under Mexican rule and the role of Anglo-American settlers and Tejanos.
- ELO: Describe the key players involved in the early stages of the conflict, including Texian leaders, Mexican officials, and cultural groups.
- ELO: Analyze the major battles and political actions during the war, such as the Siege of the Alamo, the Texas Declaration of Independence, and the Battle of San Jacinto.
- ELO: Evaluate the establishment and constitution of the Republic of Texas, including its policies on slavery and rights of different groups.
- ELO: Compare differing perspectives on the motivations and legitimacy of the Texas War of Independence, addressing field disagreements and cultural dynamics.

## Key Terms
- texas war of independence: A conflict from October 1835 to April 1836 in which Texian settlers and Tejanos fought to gain independence from Mexican rule. Why it matters: It marks the creation of the Republic of Texas and shaped the political landscape of the region.
- texian: A term used to describe Anglo-American settlers living in Texas during the period of Mexican rule and the Texas Revolution. Why it matters: Understanding who Texians were helps explain the cultural and political tensions leading to the war.
- tejano: A Mexican-born resident of Texas who lived in the region before and during the Texas War of Independence. Why it matters: Tejanos played key roles in the conflict and their perspectives add depth to the historical narrative.
- empresario land grants: Contracts given by the Mexican government to individuals (empresarios) to bring settlers to Texas under conditions like Mexican citizenship and Catholicism. Why it matters: These grants led to increased Anglo-American settlement, which contributed to tensions with Mexico.
- siege of the alamo: A pivotal 1836 battle where Mexican forces besieged Texian defenders at the Alamo mission in San Antonio, resulting in Texian defeat. Why it matters: The battle became a symbol of Texian resistance and inspired further support for independence.
- battle of san jacinto: The decisive 1836 battle where Texian forces led by Sam Houston defeated Mexican troops and captured General Santa Anna. Why it matters: This victory secured Texian independence and ended major fighting in the war.
- texas declaration of independence: A document adopted on March 2, 1836, declaring Texas independent from Mexico, primarily drafted by George Childress. Why it matters: It formally announced Texas’s break from Mexico and justified the revolution.
- sam houston: A key Texian military leader and politician who led forces at San Jacinto and later became president of the Republic of Texas. Why it matters: His leadership was crucial to Texian victory and the establishment of Texas as an independent republic.
- republic of texas: The independent nation established after the Texas War of Independence, with a constitution modeled on the U.S. but including protections for slavery. Why it matters: It represents the political outcome of the war and influenced future U.S. expansion.
- centralist vs. federalist conflict: Political struggle in Mexico between centralists who favored strong central government and federalists who supported states’ rights. Why it matters: This conflict affected Texas’s political status and contributed to the outbreak of the war.
