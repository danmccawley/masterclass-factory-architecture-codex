## Key Terms
- salem witch trials: A series of hearings and prosecutions in 1692 in Salem Village, Massachusetts, where over 150 people were accused of witchcraft, resulting in 19 executions before the hysteria ended. Why it matters: Understanding this event provides the historical context for the Ingalls family's potential involvement and the social dynamics of the period.
- ingalls family: A family line in 17th-century Massachusetts, with some members, notably Martha Ingalls Allen Carrier, linked to the witch trials through accusations and trial records. Why it matters: Clarifying who the Ingalls were is essential to accurately assess their role and avoid conflating different individuals or family lines.
- martha ingalls allen carrier: A documented member of the Ingalls family accused, tried, and executed for witchcraft in Andover in 1692, with her case illustrating social tensions of the time. Why it matters: Her case provides concrete evidence of the Ingalls family's direct involvement in the witch trials.
- primary sources: Original documents or records from the time of the witch trials, such as court transcripts, warrants, and confessions. Why it matters: They offer direct evidence but require careful analysis to avoid bias and misinterpretation.
- secondary sources: Later interpretations, analyses, or retellings of the witch trials based on primary sources or other research. Why it matters: They help contextualize events but may include bias, dramatization, or family lore, necessitating cautious evaluation.
- puritan beliefs: Religious doctrines held by the Salem community that viewed witchcraft as a pact with the devil, influencing legal and social responses to accusations. Why it matters: Understanding these beliefs is key to interpreting the motivations and procedures of the witch trials.
- genealogical verification: The process of confirming family relationships and identities through historical records to accurately link individuals to events. Why it matters: It prevents confusion caused by common names and ensures accurate historical accounts of the Ingalls family.
- historical uncertainty: The limitations and gaps in surviving records, conflicting accounts, and biases that make some historical conclusions tentative. Why it matters: Recognizing uncertainty helps avoid overstating claims about the Ingalls family's role and promotes evidence-based narratives.
- indemnity acts: Legislative measures passed after the trials that annulled guilty verdicts and compensated families of the accused, including those related to the Ingalls family. Why it matters: They reflect official recognition of wrongful convictions and affect the historical legacy of the trials.
- source criticism: The practice of evaluating the reliability, bias, and context of historical sources to discern fact from opinion or myth. Why it matters: Essential for building cautious, evidence-based accounts of the Ingalls family's involvement in the witch trials.
- terminal learning objective: The main capability learners should demonstrate by the end of the class. Why it matters: It anchors the lesson around outcomes instead of loose topic coverage.
- enabling learning objective: A supporting skill or idea learners need before they can meet the terminal objective. Why it matters: It turns a big outcome into teachable steps.
- source verification: An independent check that claims and citations are supported by the approved corpus. Why it matters: It keeps fabricated or unsupported material out of the class.
