## Learning Objectives
- TLO: By the end of this masterclass, learners will be able to describe the basic steps and essential ingredients involved in baking a simple loaf of bread, recognizing the limitations of current evidence and identifying key areas requiring further research.
- ELO: Identify and list common ingredients used in bread baking and their roles.
- ELO: Explain the general process of mixing, kneading, fermenting, and baking dough.
- ELO: Recognize common challenges and troubleshooting tips in bread baking based on provisional knowledge.
- ELO: Understand the importance of evidence-based practices and the current gaps in bread baking knowledge.

## Course Blueprint
- 1. Orientation and learner baseline (2 slides): Set the purpose, audience floor, assumptions, and mastery target.
- 2. Knowledge base and source boundary (3 slides): Show what the approved sources support, what is missing, and what must not be invented.
- 3. Core concepts and vocabulary (5 slides): Teach the essential terms, mental models, and decision points.

- 6. Assessment, transfer, and works cited (7 slides): Prove mastery, capture participation, and close with source transparency.

## Key Terms
- bread baking: The process of making bread by mixing ingredients, kneading dough, fermenting, shaping, and baking it. Why it matters: Understanding bread baking is essential to produce edible bread and to appreciate the steps involved despite current knowledge gaps.
- ingredients: The basic components used in bread baking, typically including flour, water, yeast, and salt. Why it matters: Knowing ingredients and their roles helps learners grasp what is needed to make bread and how each affects the final product.
- kneading: The process of working dough by hand or machine to develop gluten and create elasticity. Why it matters: Kneading is crucial for dough structure and texture, impacting bread quality.
- fermentation: The stage where yeast consumes sugars in the dough, producing gases that cause the dough to rise. Why it matters: Fermentation affects bread flavor, texture, and volume, making it a key step in baking.
- proofing: The final rise of shaped dough before baking, allowing it to expand and develop flavor. Why it matters: Proper proofing ensures good bread volume and crumb structure.
- troubleshooting: Identifying and solving common problems in bread baking, such as dense texture or poor rise. Why it matters: Troubleshooting helps bakers improve results despite limited evidence on best practices.
- evidence-based practice: Using verified and credible sources to guide bread baking techniques and decisions. Why it matters: It ensures reliability and effectiveness in baking, highlighting the need for more research in this field.
- dough: A mixture of flour, water, yeast, and other ingredients before baking. Why it matters: Understanding dough properties is fundamental to controlling bread quality.
- terminal learning objective: The main capability learners should demonstrate by the end of the class. Why it matters: It anchors the lesson around outcomes instead of loose topic coverage.
- enabling learning objective: A supporting skill or idea learners need before they can meet the terminal objective. Why it matters: It turns a big outcome into teachable steps.
- source verification: An independent check that claims and citations are supported by the approved corpus. Why it matters: It keeps fabricated or unsupported material out of the class.
