## Key Terms
- texican: An Anglo-American settler living in Mexican Texas between 1820 and 1836, during which Texas was governed by Mexico and colonization was promoted through land grants and empresario contracts. Why it matters: Understanding 'Texican' clarifies the identity and cultural background of early settlers who influenced Texas's political and social development before independence.
- texian: A term used during the Republic of Texas era (1836–1845) to describe residents of Texas, reflecting the political shift from Mexican rule to an independent republic. Why it matters: Recognizing 'Texian' helps distinguish the changing political identities and allegiances in Texas history following the revolution.
- texan: The modern term for residents of Texas, adopted after Texas became a U.S. state in 1845, encompassing a broader and more diverse population than earlier terms. Why it matters: Knowing 'Texan' situates the evolution of regional identity from colonial times to statehood and beyond.
- empresario: A land agent or contractor authorized by the Mexican government to settle families in Texas by granting land, such as Stephen F. Austin who established large colonies. Why it matters: Empresarios were key to Texican settlement patterns and shaped the demographic and economic landscape of early Texas.
- land grant: A parcel of land given by the Mexican government to settlers or empresarios to encourage colonization and development in Texas. Why it matters: Land grants were the primary incentive for Anglo-American immigration and settlement, influencing Texican lifestyle and territorial claims.
- texas revolution: The armed conflict (1835–1836) between Texican settlers and the Mexican government that led to Texas gaining independence from Mexico. Why it matters: The revolution marks the pivotal political and military role of Texicans in shaping Texas’s future as an independent republic.
- caddo and comanche: Native American tribes inhabiting Texas during the Texican period, with whom settlers had complex interactions including conflict and negotiation. Why it matters: Understanding these tribes is essential to grasp the cultural and frontier dynamics influencing Texican settlement and identity.
- mexican colonization policies: Laws and regulations enacted by Mexico to encourage settlement in Texas, including requirements for settlers to swear loyalty to Mexico and adopt Christianity. Why it matters: These policies shaped who became Texicans and influenced tensions leading to the Texas Revolution.
