## Key Terms
- texican: An Anglo-American settler living in Mexican Texas between 1820 and 1836, who settled under Mexican rule by accepting land grants and swearing loyalty to Mexico. Why it matters: Understanding 'Texican' clarifies the early cultural and political identity of settlers before Texas independence, distinguishing them from later 'Texians' and 'Texans'.
- texian: A term used during the Republic of Texas era (1836–1845) to describe residents of Texas after independence from Mexico but before U.S. statehood. Why it matters: Recognizing 'Texian' helps track the political evolution of Texas residents and their shifting identities post-independence.
- texan: A resident of Texas after it became a U.S. state in 1845, reflecting the modern political and cultural identity of the region's inhabitants. Why it matters: Distinguishing 'Texan' from earlier terms shows how Texas identity evolved with changing sovereignty and governance.
- empresario: An individual authorized by the Mexican government to recruit settlers and manage land grants in Mexican Texas, such as Stephen F. Austin. Why it matters: Empresarios were key to Texican settlement patterns and frontier development, shaping early Texas demographics and land ownership.
- texas revolution: The armed conflict (1835–1836) where Texicans and others fought against Mexican rule, leading to Texas independence and the Republic of Texas. Why it matters: The revolution marks a pivotal moment in Texican history, highlighting their role in shaping Texas’s political future.
- treaty of guadalupe hidalgo: The 1848 treaty ending the Mexican-American War, through which Mexico ceded Texas and other territories to the United States. Why it matters: This treaty transformed Texican political status and identity by incorporating Texas into the U.S., influencing subsequent cultural shifts.
- native american tribes in texas: Indigenous groups such as the Comanche and Caddo who inhabited Texas and interacted with Texicans through trade, conflict, and cultural exchange. Why it matters: Their presence shaped Texican settlement patterns and frontier life, making them essential to understanding regional history.
- land grant system: A Mexican government policy granting large tracts of land to settlers and empresarios to encourage colonization of Texas. Why it matters: This system facilitated Texican settlement and economic development but also led to tensions with Native Americans and Mexican authorities.
