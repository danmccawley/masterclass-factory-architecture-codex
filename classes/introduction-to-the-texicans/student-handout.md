## Learning Objectives
- TLO: Identify who the Texicans were and describe their cultural background, including their origins, settlement patterns, and historical role in early Texas.
- ELO: Define the term 'Texican' and explain its etymological origins and cultural significance.
- ELO: Discuss the settlement patterns, geographic influences, and lifestyle characteristics of the Texicans in early Texas.
- ELO: Explain the role of the Texicans in early Texas history, including their participation in the Texas Revolution and the formation of the Republic of Texas.

## Key Terms
- texican: A historical term referring to early settlers of Texas who embodied a cultural blend of Anglo-American and Tejano (Mexican-Texan) influences. Why it matters: Understanding 'Texican' is key to grasping the unique cultural identity and heritage that shaped early Texas society and its diverse population.
- tejano: A person of Mexican descent born or living in Texas, often associated with the Spanish-speaking communities before and during Texas' early history. Why it matters: Tejanos represent a foundational cultural group whose traditions and interactions influenced Texican identity and Texas history.
- texas revolution: The armed conflict (1835–1836) between Texan settlers and the Mexican government that led to Texas gaining independence and forming the Republic of Texas. Why it matters: The revolution was a pivotal event where Texicans played significant roles, shaping the political future and identity of Texas.
- republic of texas: An independent nation from 1836 to 1845 established after Texas won independence from Mexico, before joining the United States. Why it matters: The Republic era reflects Texican political aspirations and governance, marking a distinct period in Texas history.
- spanish missions: Religious and military outposts established by Spain in Texas to spread Christianity and secure territorial claims during colonial times. Why it matters: These missions influenced Texican settlement patterns and cultural exchanges between indigenous peoples, Mexicans, and settlers.
- treaty of guadalupe hidalgo: The 1848 treaty that ended the Mexican-American War, defining the U.S.-Mexico border and impacting territorial claims including Texas. Why it matters: This treaty legally shaped Texican identity and land ownership by formalizing borders and sovereignty.
- anglo-american settlers: English-speaking settlers from the United States who migrated to Texas during Mexican rule and contributed to its cultural and political landscape. Why it matters: Their migration and interactions with Tejanos helped form the Texican cultural blend and influenced Texas' path to independence.
- texas essential knowledge and skills (teks): The official Texas state standards outlining educational objectives, including those related to Texas history and culture. Why it matters: TEKS provides a primary framework for teaching about Texicans and their historical context in Texas education.
- terminal learning objective: The main capability learners should demonstrate by the end of the class. Why it matters: It anchors the lesson around outcomes instead of loose topic coverage.
- enabling learning objective: A supporting skill or idea learners need before they can meet the terminal objective. Why it matters: It turns a big outcome into teachable steps.
- source verification: An independent check that claims and citations are supported by the approved corpus. Why it matters: It keeps fabricated or unsupported material out of the class.
