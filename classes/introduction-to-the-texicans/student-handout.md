## Learning Objectives
- TLO: Identify who the Texicans were and describe their cultural background, settlement patterns, and their role in early Texas history.
- ELO: Define the term 'Texican' and explain its historical origins and usage.
- ELO: Describe the cultural background of the Texicans, including Spanish, Mexican, indigenous, and European influences.
- ELO: Analyze the settlement patterns and lifestyle of the Texicans on the Texas frontier.
- ELO: Explain the role and perspectives of the Texicans in key historical events such as the Texas Revolution and the transition from Spanish to Mexican rule.

## Key Terms
- texican: A historical term referring to settlers in Texas of Spanish or Mexican descent who lived during the period of Spanish and Mexican rule and played key roles in early Texas history. Why it matters: Understanding 'Texican' clarifies the distinct cultural and historical identity of these early settlers, differentiating them from Anglo-American Texans and highlighting their influence on Texas’s development.
- texas revolution: A conflict from 1835 to 1836 in which Texicans, Anglo settlers, and others fought to gain independence from Mexico, leading to the creation of the Republic of Texas. Why it matters: The Texas Revolution was a pivotal event shaping Texican political and military roles and the future of Texas as an independent republic and later a U.S. state.
- spanish colonial heritage: The cultural, social, and legal traditions inherited from Spain’s colonization of Texas, influencing language, religion, land use, and governance among Texicans. Why it matters: This heritage forms a core part of Texican identity, explaining their customs, settlement patterns, and interactions with indigenous peoples and newcomers.
- mexican rule in texas: The period from 1821 to 1836 when Texas was part of Mexico following independence from Spain, during which Texicans navigated shifting political and social dynamics. Why it matters: Understanding this era is crucial to grasp the Texicans’ changing loyalties, cultural adaptations, and involvement in conflicts leading to Texas independence.
- settlement patterns: The ways Texicans established communities, including locations, land use, housing, and social organization on the Texas frontier. Why it matters: Studying settlement patterns reveals how Texicans adapted to the environment, maintained cultural traditions, and interacted with indigenous and Anglo populations.
- primary sources: Original documents, letters, artifacts, and records from the time of the Texicans that provide firsthand evidence of their lives and history. Why it matters: Primary sources are essential for accurate, nuanced understanding of Texican identity and history, allowing learners to analyze perspectives beyond secondary interpretations.
- anglo-american settlers: English-speaking settlers from the United States who moved into Texas during Mexican rule, often leading to cultural and political tensions with Texicans. Why it matters: Recognizing this group helps explain the complex social dynamics and conflicts that shaped Texican experiences and Texas history.
- indigenous peoples of texas: Native tribes and communities living in Texas before and during Texican settlement, influencing and interacting with Texican culture and society. Why it matters: Their presence and relationships with Texicans are key to understanding cultural exchanges, conflicts, and the frontier lifestyle.
