## Learning Objectives
- TLO: Analyze the causes, key events, and outcomes of the Texas War of Independence, including its major battles, political developments, and aftermath.
- TLO: Evaluate the roles and perspectives of significant figures and factions involved in the Texas Revolution, considering Texan, Mexican, and indigenous viewpoints.
- TLO: Interpret primary source documents related to the Texas War of Independence to understand the historical context, political debates, and social impacts of the conflict.
- ELO: Describe the political and social conditions in Texas and Mexico leading up to the Texas War of Independence.
- ELO: Identify and explain the significance of major battles such as the Siege of the Alamo, Goliad Campaign, and Battle of San Jacinto.
- ELO: Summarize the content and implications of the Texas Declaration of Independence and the establishment of the Republic of Texas.
- ELO: Recognize key individuals involved in the conflict and their contributions or roles.
- ELO: Analyze the role of slavery and other contentious political issues within the Texas Revolution.
- ELO: Examine primary source materials including government records, correspondence, and maps to gain firsthand insights into the war.
- ELO: Discuss the social and cultural impact of the war on indigenous peoples and Tejanos, acknowledging gaps in historical records.
- ELO: Compare and contrast Texan and Mexican perspectives on the revolution, noting areas of historical disagreement.

## Key Terms
- texas war of independence: A conflict from October 2, 1835, to April 21, 1836, in which Texan settlers and Tejanos fought to gain independence from Mexico. Why it matters: It marks the birth of the Republic of Texas and shaped the region's political future.
- siege of the alamo: A pivotal 13-day battle in 1836 where Mexican forces besieged Texan defenders at the Alamo mission in San Antonio. Why it matters: The Alamo became a symbol of Texan resistance and sacrifice during the revolution.
- battle of san jacinto: The decisive battle on April 21, 1836, where Texan forces led by Sam Houston defeated General Santa Anna’s army. Why it matters: This victory secured Texan independence from Mexico.
- texas declaration of independence: The document adopted on March 2, 1836, declaring Texas independent from Mexico and establishing a provisional government. Why it matters: It formalized the Texan cause and set the foundation for the Republic of Texas.
- sam houston: Commander-in-chief of Texan forces during the revolution and later the first president of the Republic of Texas. Why it matters: His leadership was crucial to Texan victory and early governance.
- antonio lópez de santa anna: Mexican general and president who led the Mexican army against Texan rebels during the war. Why it matters: His defeat marked the end of Mexican control over Texas.
- republic of texas: The independent nation established after the Texas War of Independence, existing from 1836 to 1845. Why it matters: It represents Texas’s brief period as a sovereign country before joining the United States.
- tejanos: Mexican residents of Texas who often supported Texan independence but had complex loyalties during the revolution. Why it matters: Their perspectives highlight the diverse social and cultural dynamics of the conflict.
- slavery in texas revolution: The institution of slavery was protected in the Texan constitution despite Mexican laws banning it, creating political tension. Why it matters: Understanding this issue is key to grasping the social and political conflicts underlying the revolution.
- primary sources: Original documents, letters, maps, and records from the time of the Texas War of Independence. Why it matters: They provide firsthand evidence crucial for accurate historical interpretation.
- terminal learning objective: The main capability learners should demonstrate by the end of the class. Why it matters: It anchors the lesson around outcomes instead of loose topic coverage.
- enabling learning objective: A supporting skill or idea learners need before they can meet the terminal objective. Why it matters: It turns a big outcome into teachable steps.
- source verification: An independent check that claims and citations are supported by the approved corpus. Why it matters: It keeps fabricated or unsupported material out of the class.
