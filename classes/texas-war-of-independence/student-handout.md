# Student Handout: Texas War of Independence

Class tier: Standard class
Bernard support: ask Bernard for clarification, examples, translation, quiz help, or a deeper explanation. Bernard must stay inside the approved source boundary.

## Learning Objectives
- TLO: Analyze the causes, key events, and outcomes of the Texas War of Independence, demonstrating an understanding of the political, social, and military factors that shaped the revolution and its aftermath.
- ELO: Describe the political context in Mexico leading up to the Texas Revolution, including the impact of Santa Anna's centralist policies and the 1824 Constitution.
- ELO: Identify and explain the significance of major battles and campaigns during the Texas Revolution, such as the Battle of Gonzales, the Siege of the Alamo, the Goliad Campaign, and the Battle of San Jacinto.
- ELO: Examine the roles and perspectives of different groups involved in the revolution, including Anglo-American settlers, Tejanos, enslaved African Americans, and Mexican residents.
- ELO: Interpret primary source documents related to the Texas Revolution from archives and historical commissions to understand contemporary viewpoints and government actions.
- ELO: Evaluate the political and social consequences of the Texas Declaration of Independence and the establishment of the provisional government under Sam Houston.
- ELO: Discuss the internal divisions among Texans and the complexities of loyalty and identity during the revolution.
- ELO: Assess the limitations and biases in secondary sources and the importance of corroborating contested historical interpretations with primary evidence.
- ELO: Apply critical thinking to understand the broader implications of the Texas Revolution on regional politics and its legacy in American and Mexican history.

## Key Terms
- texas revolution: A conflict from October 1835 to April 1836 in which Texian settlers fought to gain independence from Mexico. Why it matters: It marks the struggle that led to the creation of the Republic of Texas and shaped regional history.
- battle of gonzales: The first military engagement of the Texas Revolution, where Texians resisted Mexican troops attempting to retrieve a cannon. Why it matters: It symbolizes the start of armed resistance and Texian determination for independence.
- siege of the alamo: A 13-day siege in early 1836 where Mexican forces attacked the Texian-held Alamo mission, resulting in Texian defeat. Why it matters: The Alamo became a rallying point and symbol of Texian sacrifice and resolve.
- battle of san jacinto: The decisive battle in April 1836 where Texian forces led by Sam Houston defeated the Mexican army, securing independence. Why it matters: It effectively ended the war and ensured Texas's de facto independence from Mexico.
- santa anna: The Mexican president and general whose centralist policies and military actions triggered the Texas Revolution. Why it matters: Understanding his role explains the political causes and escalation of the conflict.
- 1824 mexican constitution: A federalist constitution that granted significant autonomy to Mexican states, including Texas before Santa Anna's centralist shift. Why it matters: Its overthrow alienated Texans and other federalists, fueling revolutionary sentiment.
- anglo-american settlers: Primarily English-speaking settlers from the United States who moved to Texas, many bringing enslaved people despite Mexican laws. Why it matters: They were a major demographic driving the revolution and influenced its social and political dynamics.
- tejanos: Mexican residents of Texas who had lived there before Anglo settlement and had varied loyalties during the revolution. Why it matters: Their perspectives highlight internal divisions and the complexity of identity in the conflict.
- texas declaration of independence: The formal document adopted on March 2, 1836, declaring Texas independent from Mexico and establishing a provisional government. Why it matters: It marks the official political break and foundation of the Republic of Texas.
- sam houston: Commander-in-chief of Texian forces and first president of the Republic of Texas. Why it matters: His leadership was crucial to Texian military success and early governance.
- provisional government: The temporary government established by Texians during the revolution to organize resistance and governance. Why it matters: It provided political structure amid conflict and laid groundwork for independence.
- goliad campaign: A series of battles and events including the capture and execution of Texian prisoners by Mexican forces in 1836. Why it matters: It intensified Texian resolve and became a rallying cause during the revolution.
- federalism vs. centralism: The political conflict between Mexican states' desire for autonomy (federalism) and the central government's push for control (centralism). Why it matters: This conflict underpinned tensions leading to the Texas Revolution.
- primary sources: Original documents, letters, government records, and firsthand accounts from the time of the Texas Revolution. Why it matters: They provide direct evidence and perspectives essential for accurate historical understanding.
- internal divisions: Differences in loyalty, identity, and goals among Anglo settlers, Tejanos, enslaved African Americans, and Mexican residents during the revolution. Why it matters: Recognizing these divisions reveals the social complexity and contested nature of the conflict.
- terminal learning objective: The main capability learners should demonstrate by the end of the class. Why it matters: It anchors the lesson around outcomes instead of loose topic coverage.
- enabling learning objective: A supporting skill or idea learners need before they can meet the terminal objective. Why it matters: It turns a big outcome into teachable steps.
- source verification: An independent check that claims and citations are supported by the approved corpus. Why it matters: It keeps fabricated or unsupported material out of the class.
